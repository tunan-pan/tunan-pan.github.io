Seattle Animal Shelter:

Helping animal lovers complete their donations

Case study 

The Problem

Seattle Animal Shelter’s online donation experience has several friction points: low CTA visibility, an abrupt external payment transition with key donation options hidden behind this link, and a long multi-step form that increases cognitive load.

These issues likely reduce completion rates, especially for first-time donors coming from external channels, who have little patience for friction or uncertainty about trust.

The pathway to donating from the home page is also very indirect and low emphasis, but I decided to focus specifically on improving the donation flow after users reach the donation page.

[original page screenshot]

My Design Goals

Increase successful donation completion by:

  • Reducing drop-off during donation start and checkout transition
  • Improving perceived trust in external payment step
  • Reducing cognitive load during donation entry and form completion

The Constraints

  • Government website structure and design limitations
  • External payment system (.aspx)
  • Likely limited engineering resources

These constraints led me to focus on flow and structure rather than visual redesign.

Researching Competitors and Best Practices

I wanted to understand whether the friction points I'd identified were specific to SAS or reflected broader patterns in donation UX, and what the most effective flows had in common.

I researched donation and general shopping/payment best practices, and reviewed donation flows across several similar organizations: a government shelter, well known shelters and animal organizations, and other shelters/rescues in the local Washington area. 

Key Insights

Reducing friction

  • Multi-step flows are commonly used in donation experiences and reduce cognitive load, making them more effective than single long forms (additionally, it supports progressive data capture that allows the organization to re-engage users who do drop off)
  • Strong visual hierarchy and minimal initial steps help users begin interacting quickly
  • Compelling imagery and messaging can increase motivation to complete a donation

Trust & clarity

  • Consistent branding reinforces trust throughout the process, especially when redirected to an external page
  • Progress indicators help set expectations for the length of the process

Decision support

  • Default amounts and monthly giving options reduce hesitation
  • Tangible, impact-based messaging (e.g. “$25 fills a food bowl”) supports decision-making
  • Visual cues (e.g. heart icons and subtle animations) can guide users toward preferred actions

Some of these best practices (impact-based messaging, monthly giving options, and visual delight moments) I didn’t include, due to SAS's existing content structure and likely engineering constraints.

Defining success

These are the metrics I would use to measure success:

  • Donation completion rate
  • Form drop-off rate
  • Time to complete donation

While baseline metrics are unavailable, these KPIs reflect the primary friction points identified in the current experience. Informal testing suggests the current flow takes approximately ___ minutes to complete.

User flow

I mapped the current and proposed donation flows to identify key drop-off points and opportunities to reduce friction. This analysis directly informed the restructuring of the donation flow and prioritization of key interactions.

Key friction points in current flow:

  • Low visibility of donation entry point
  • Unclear external transition
  • High cognitive load in long form structure
  • Decision fatigue in multiple funds selection
  • Unexpected multi-step process increases abandonment risk

How the proposed flow addresses this:

  • Immediate access to donation options
  • Donation amounts are visible above the fold, reducing time to action
  • Informed transition to secure payment
  • Simplified, restructured three-step form with clear progress indicator

Design Decisions

During wireframing, I focused on key interaction decisions that would reduce friction and improve clarity in the donation process. There were two areas where I tried to think outside the box a little to better fit SAS’s content, but realized they were impractical:

Structuring the initial donation decision

I explored whether users should select a donation fund before choosing an amount, which would allow for more emotionally compelling storytelling through imagery. But since many donors arrive with a set amount in mind, requiring a more involved choice upfront would increase friction, so I prioritized donation amount as the first step to keep the initial interaction fast and low-effort.

Choosing a familiar multi-step flow

I considered a single-page accordion-style form to reduce page loads and streamline the experience. However, since this pattern is uncommon in donation flows, I chose a conventional multi-step structure with clear progress indicators to maintain familiarity and trust, and reduce cognitive load.

Final Flow & Design

[images of 4 main screens]

Key UI Decisions

Reduced initial friction

Donation amount selection on landing page, with preset options, visible above the fold. Compelling image to further motivate users.

Reduced cognitive load

Original copy has been condensed, with tax and privacy details hidden behind a link, while mailing details are moved to the “Other ways to donate” section.

Improved trust during transition

Users are informed before redirecting to the external payment system. The external page will also open in the same window instead of a new tab.

Simplified decision-making

Limited to single fund selection, and each fund renamed for clarity, with full descriptions available via tooltip.

Clear progress and structure

A three-step flow separates donation, billing, and payment into focused tasks, with clear progress indicators. On mobile, the continue button is anchored to the bottom for thumb-friendly access.
